Learn About Medical Food Deductions

Did you know you can deduct part of the extra cost of medically necessary foods on your taxes every year? Many people spend hundreds or even thousands of dollars extra annually on gluten-free, low-sodium, keto, allergy-specific, or prescribed diets — and don’t realize that portion of the additional cost may qualify as a medical expense deduction under IRS rules.

This page explains the basics, which diets and foods might qualify, and how DeductEats makes tracking and documenting everything much easier.

What the IRS Allows

The IRS allows you to include in medical expenses the extra cost of special foods if they are used to treat or alleviate a specific medical condition. The key requirements are:

  • The food must be a special diet that does not satisfy ordinary nutritional needs.
  • The food must alleviate or treat an illness or condition.
  • The need for the food must be substantiated by a physician (usually a written statement or prescription).

Only the amount by which the special food exceeds the cost of a normal diet can be deducted. For example, if gluten-free bread costs $6 per loaf and regular bread costs $3, only the $3 difference may qualify.

Real-world savings vary widely depending on your diet, grocery habits, and medical needs, but many people who qualify report deducting anywhere from $200 to $2,000+ per year in extra food costs — sometimes more for families or strict prescribed diets. The deduction only applies to the portion of total medical expenses (including food) that exceeds 7.5% of your adjusted gross income (AGI), and you must itemize on Schedule A.

Read the full IRS Publication 502 (Medical and Dental Expenses)

Diets That May Qualify

Qualification depends on your individual medical condition and physician documentation. Common diets that are sometimes deductible (when medically necessary) include:

  • Gluten-Free Diet — Often prescribed for celiac disease, dermatitis herpetiformis, or severe non-celiac gluten sensitivity with medical complications.
  • Low-Sodium Diet — Commonly recommended for high blood pressure, heart failure, kidney disease, or liver conditions requiring sodium restriction.
  • Ketogenic (Keto) Diet — May qualify for certain types of intractable epilepsy (especially in children) or specific metabolic disorders under medical supervision.
  • Allergy-Specific Diets — Such as dairy-free (severe lactose intolerance or milk allergy), nut-free (life-threatening nut allergies), or other strict allergen avoidance diets when medically required.
  • Other Prescribed Diets — Examples include low-FODMAP for IBS, PKU diet for phenylketonuria, or other highly specialized diets.

Many other diets may also qualify if a physician documents that they are medically necessary to treat a specific condition and exceed normal nutritional needs. The key is always medical substantiation — not general health or preference.
